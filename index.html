01 Context

The world's fastest growing sport. Barely here.

Padel is the world's fastest growing sport. Growing up in Dubai, I watched it take over. When I arrived in Canada, padel was barely here. But the few places that had courts were completely booked out. The demand signal was obvious. Supply was the constraint, not interest.

In Kitchener-Waterloo: one facility, one court, one owner. No student presence. Nobody had tried to build the demand side. That gap is what I went looking for.

02 The Data

I scraped their booking data. The gap was hiding in plain sight.

Rather than assume, I pulled booking data directly from Rookie and Ace's reservation system. The pattern was immediate: courts sat near-empty every weekday before 3:30pm — a multi-hour window of dead time on a fixed-cost asset.

I also interviewed Derek directly to understand the cost structure. Well-managed Canadian clubs typically run at 35–50% utilization. Rookie and Ace was below that benchmark, concentrated entirely in the evening.

03 Affordability Problem

Padel is an expensive sport. That's half the problem.

Even if courts were available during the day, students couldn't afford the market rate. The solution had to fix both sides: unlock the supply and lower the price point. One without the other doesn't work.

The negotiated student rate — $25+HST including rackets — ended up cheaper than Rookie and Ace's own off-peak split rate, with equipment included. That's the structural unlock.

04 The Intervention

Not a pitch. A proposal backed by evidence.

I approached Derek with the data, not an ask. The proposal solved two problems simultaneously: his utilization gap and the affordability barrier keeping students off the court. The result was a formal written agreement, signed and operational within weeks.

09 What I Learned

The method is the same everywhere.

Find the gap between what exists and what should exist. Diagnose it with data. Propose a solution that works for both sides. Take responsibility before you have the title to justify it.

That's product thinking: identifying a two-sided problem, building a solution that serves both sides simultaneously, and measuring it against real outcomes. I did it with a booking spreadsheet and a padel racket. The instinct scales.

I can't see a broken system and leave it alone. And I don't rely on instinct to diagnose it — I pull the data first, because that's what earns the conversation. That's not something I decided to be. It's just what happens when I look at something long enough.

Pattern 01
Data changes the conversation
I didn't pitch Derek on enthusiasm. I showed him his own numbers in a way he hadn't seen before. That's what made the partnership possible, not the idea itself.
Pattern 02
I use data to find undervalued segments
Students were an undervalued segment Derek hadn't priced or marketed toward. That's not just a padel insight — it's the same analytical instinct I applied at TD. The context changes. The method doesn't.
Pattern 03
Both sides of the market matter
Solving Derek's utilization gap alone doesn't work if students can't afford to show up. The affordability piece wasn't secondary. It was half the solution.
Pattern 04
Ownership without permission
Nobody asked me to fix this. I just couldn't walk past it once I saw it. That's not a personality trait I'm describing. It's a pattern I keep returning to.
